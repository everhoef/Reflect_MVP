Stage 1: Happiness Histogram (Check-in)
Why are we doing this?
We’re starting with a quick exercise to check the emotional pulse of the team. By gauging how happy or frustrated everyone feels, we can open the door to meaningful discussions about what’s influencing our mood—both the good and the bad. 

What do we expect from you?
You’ll be asked to rate your happiness on a scale from 1 to 10 and explain why you chose that number. This helps everyone understand where we’re at emotionally and what might need our attention.


1. Facilitator explanation
Hi everyone!
We’re starting our retrospective with a Happiness Histogram. This is a great way to see how everyone is feeling about the work, the team, or the project in general. Here’s how we’ll do it:

Check out the link I’ve shared. You’ll see a dropdown menu where you can select your happiness level, from 1 (Unhappy) to 10 (Happy).

(Screen must show the different Lanes)
Rate your happiness on a scale from 1 to 10

Reflect and choose Your Score  
  Take a moment to think about your experience. Pick the number that feels right to you. 
  - A higher number means you’re feeling positive, excited, or generally happy. 
  - A lower number might indicate frustration, stress, or other concerns. 
  Remember, there’s no right or wrong answer—this is about *your* feelings.

Share a short (optional) explanation  
  After selecting your score, there’s a text box for you to add a quick explanation. Why did you pick that number? 
  - For example: “I’m at a 9 because I feel we’ve achieved a lot this sprint!” 
  - Or: “I’m at a 4 because I’ve been struggling to balance priorities.” 
  Keep it brief but clear, so others can understand your perspective.

2. User action
Select their happiness score and submit their response. This result is not yet shared with the team (everyone votes individually / anonymously)

3. Facilitator explanation
(Screen must now show overview of all the submitted votes and who voted what)
Here’s what to do next.
Once everyone has submitted their scores, I’ll share the overview page with the results.

Observe the Overview  
  Take a few moments to look at the histogram. You’ll see how the team’s scores are distributed. 
  - Are the scores clustered in one area, or are they spread out? 
  - Are there any surprising patterns or notable outliers? 

Reflect on the Results  
  Look for anything that resonates with you. Are there scores or comments that stand out? Does anyone’s explanation prompt questions or reflections? 

4. User action
Discuss as Needed  
  In the comments section of the shared board (or in the designated discussion space), users add their thoughts like: 
  - “I noticed many people scored a 7 or 8—this feels like good momentum.” 
  - “I’m curious about someone’s score of 3—what’s behind that?” 

If there’s something specific, you'd like us to discuss as a group, highlight it here. We’ll decide whether to address it now or save it for a later step in the retrospective.

5. Facilitator explanation
To wrap up this step, I’d like each of you to share (by voice-over) one insight or observation from the histogram. This could be: 
- Something that stood out to you. 
- A question you have for the team. 
- A positive takeaway about the team’s overall mood. 

Let me know if you have any questions as you go through this process. I’ll be keeping an eye on the comments and results as they come in, so feel free to tag me if you need clarification or have an observation to share.

When the overview is finalized and everyone has had a chance to reflect, I’ll guide us to the next part. Let’s get started!

6. User action
Users share their thoughts on the histogram and any patterns they noticed.

7. Facilitator explanation
Once everyone has shared their final thought, we’ll move on to the next step of our retrospective.

 
 
Stage 2: Mad, Sad, Glad (Gather Data)
Why are we doing this?  
In this stage, we’ll collect information about what happened during the sprint or project. The goal is to capture both positive and challenging moments, so we have a shared understanding of the big picture. 

What do I expect from you?  
We’ll ask you to reflect on the last sprint (or relevant timeframe) and share specific events or observations. This could include successes, struggles, surprises, or patterns you noticed.

1. Facilitator explaination
Hi everyone, let’s move on to the next step of our retrospective: Mad, Sad, Glad . This is where we’ll explore different emotions we’ve felt recently—things that made us frustrated (mad), disappointed (sad), or happy (glad). It’s a great way to surface what’s been going well and where there might be some challenges.

Here’s how it works:
(Screen must show the 3 columns of Mad, Sad, Glad)
  - Check Out the Board  
  I’ve set up a digital board for this activity. You’ll see three columns labeled Mad, Sad, and Glad. Each column represents one of those emotions.

  - Think About Your Experiences
  Take a few minutes to reflect on the last sprint (or time period we’re reviewing). Think about moments that stood out for you: 
    - What made you mad? Maybe it was something frustrating or a blocker that slowed you down. 
    - What made you sad? This could be a disappointment or something that didn’t go as planned. 
    - What made you glad? These are the wins, the moments that made you smile or feel proud. 

2. User action
Users add their Sticky Notes , Take 5 Minutes to Post

  - Grab sticky notes from the board. 
  - Write down one event or moment per sticky note. Be concise but specific (e.g., “Too many last-minute changes” for Mad, or “Great teamwork during the deadline crunch” for Glad). 
  - Use a different color sticky note for each column (Mad, Sad, and Glad) to keep it visually clear. 
  - Drag your sticky notes to the column where they belong. 

3. Facilitator explanation
(Screen must make the private sticky notes readable / unblurred to all users)
  Cluster similar notes
  Once you’ve added your sticky notes, take a moment to look at what others have posted. Is there anything that resonates with you? Feel free to add a quick comment on someone else’s note if it’s something you also experienced.

4. User action
Cluster notes
After everyone has added their stickies, users group similar ones into clusters (or feel free to do this yourself if you notice overlaps). These clusters will help us see patterns more clearly. 

5. Facilitator explaination
Name the Clusters
Once we have clusters, we’ll name them as a group. What do these patterns tell us? For example, if several “Mad” notes are about unclear priorities, we might call the cluster “Unclear Goals.”

6. User action
Users debrief together
Finally, we’ll discuss what stands out: 
  - What’s surprising? What’s expected? 
  - What was difficult or fun? 
  - What patterns do we see, and what do they mean for us as a team? 
  - How can we use this information to move forward? 

That’s it! Take your time with this step—there’s no rush. When you’re ready, let’s discuss what we’ve uncovered. 

 
Stage 3: The Original Four (Generate Insights)
Why are we doing this?  
Now that we’ve collected data, it’s time to dig deeper. We’ll look for patterns, connections, and root causes to understand why things happened the way they did. This step helps us move beyond the symptoms to the real issues we can address. 

What do I expect from you?  
You’ll work with the team to analyze the data we’ve gathered. Be curious and collaborative—ask questions, challenge assumptions, and help identify insights that will guide our next steps.

1. Facilitator explaination
Alright team let’s dive into The Original Four —a classic set of questions that help us reflect on what’s working, what we’ve learned, and where we can improve. This step is all about collaboration and making sure we explore every dimension of our work.

We’ll go through the four questions one by one. Here’s how it works:

 # Question 1: What did we do well, that if we didn’t discuss, we might forget? 
1. Take a moment to think about this question. Reflect on things we did well as a team that we should celebrate or remember for next time.
2. Post your answers as keywords or short phrases in the chat. You’ll have 2 minutes to do this. For example, you might write “clear communication” or “on-time delivery.”
3. After the time is up, we’ll take 5 minutes to discuss. I’ll summarize the answers and ask for any clarification or additional thoughts.

2. User action
Users share their thoughts on what went well.

3. Facilitator explanation
  # Question 2: What did we learn? 
  1. Let’s shift our focus now. What did we learn during this sprint or project? It could be a new skill, a process improvement, or even an insight about how we work as a team.
  2. Post your thoughts in the chat, again as keywords or short phrases. You’ll have 2 minutes to share.
  3. Once the time is up, we’ll spend 5 minutes discussing what stands out. Did anything surprise us? Is there something we can apply moving forward?

4. User action
Users share their thoughts on what they learned.

5. Facilitator explanation
  # Question 3: What should we do differently next time? 
  1. Now, think about what we could improve. What didn’t go as smoothly as it could have? What changes might make us even more effective in the future?
  2. Post your suggestions in the chat. Take 2 minutes to think and share.
  3. We’ll then spend 5 minutes discussing these ideas. Which ones feel the most actionable? Are there quick wins we can implement?

6. User action
Users share their thoughts on what they could do differently next time.

7. Facilitator explanation
  # Question 4: What still puzzles us? 
  1. Finally, let’s talk about what’s still unclear. Are there questions or challenges we haven’t fully figured out yet? Is there anything we need help with or want to explore further?
  2. Post your thoughts in the chat. Again, take 2 minutes to reflect and share.
  3. We’ll wrap up this question with a 5-minute discussion, focusing on any big themes or unresolved issues.

8. User action
Users share their thoughts on what still puzzles them.

9. Facilitator explanation
Summarize the key points we’ve discussed, and we’ll move on to the next step.

10. User action
Summarize the key points from the discussion on what still puzzles us.

 
Stage 4: Circle of Questions (Decide what 2 do)
Why are we doing this?  
Insights are valuable, but action is what drives improvement. In this step, we’ll identify specific changes or experiments we want to try in the next sprint to address the issues or opportunities we uncovered. 

What do I expect from you?  
You’ll help prioritize the most impactful actions and decide on a plan as a team. Be practical and focus on things we can realistically achieve in the next sprint.


1. Facilitator explanation
Hi team! For this step, we’ll be doing the Circle of Questions —but instead of asking and answering out loud, we’ll use a virtual board to share our thoughts. This method lets us take time to reflect while still involving everyone in the conversation.

Here’s how it works:

(Screen must show a Virtual Board with a column labeled Circle of Questions. Each person will add their own contributions here)

The First Question  
I’ll kick us off by writing the first question at the top of the column. It’ll be something like: 
What’s the most important thing we should start in the next iteration?
Feel free to take a moment to think about your answer.

Let’s get started! I’ll post the first question now: 
What’s the most important thing we should start in the next iteration?

Go to the board, add your answer, and then post your own question for the next person. Let me know if you have any questions about how to participate! 

2. First user action
Users write their answer.  
When they are ready, they should have added a sticky note to the board just below the question with their answer. Should be clear and concise (e.g., “Improve sprint planning” or “Add daily check-ins”).

3. Second user action
Once the first user has posted their answer, they should write a new question for the next person (the person below you in the list or grid view on the board). The question should be related to the one the user just answered, like: 
What’s one thing we should stop doing to improve focus?
Post your question as another sticky note right below your answer.

4. User action (continued)
The next person will answer your question and then post their own related question for the following person. We’ll continue until everyone has had a chance to participate.

5. Facilitator explanation
Finding Consensus  
As responses come in, watch for patterns or common themes. If you see an idea you strongly agree with, feel free to leave a comment or reaction on the board. Once we’ve gone through the circle, we’ll look at the board together to summarize key takeaways and identify any consensus.

6. User action
Summarize the patterns / common themes

7. Facilitator Wrap-Up  
After everyone has participated, I’ll recap the major themes and ensure we capture any action items for the team to work on.
 
 
Stage 5: Feedback Door Smileys (Closure)
Why are we doing this?  
We’ll end the retrospective by reflecting on the process itself and sharing appreciation for each other. This helps us continuously improve how we collaborate and reinforces positive team dynamics. 

What do I expect from you?  
You’ll share a brief thought about the retrospective—what went well, what could be improved, or something you appreciated about a teammate.

1. Facilitator explanation
Hi team! To wrap up our retrospective, we’re going to do a quick and simple feedback exercise to see how you felt about this session. It’s called Feedback Door Smileys and it only takes a minute!

(Screen must show a virtual board with smiley faces)
Here’s how it works:

First: Share Your Smiley  
Think about how satisfied you were with today’s retrospective. Then, pick one of these three smileys to represent your feeling: 
  - :) Happy – You found the retro valuable and enjoyed it. 
  - :| Mwah – It was okay, but there’s room for improvement. 
  - :( Sad – You didn’t find it helpful or enjoyable. 

Second: Post Your Smiley  
Drop your chosen smiley in the comments section of the conference tool or wherever we’ve agreed to collect feedback (e.g., a specific chat or the virtual board).

Third: Capture the Results  
I’ll tally up the responses to see how we did. This helps me understand what worked well and what we might improve for next time.

Optional: Add a Comment  
If you want to explain your choice, feel free to add a quick comment alongside your smiley. For example: 
  - “:) Loved the structured flow!” 
  - “:| Would have liked more time for the discussion.” 
  - “:( Felt rushed during some activities.” 

 That’s it—super simple! Please go ahead and share your smiley now, and I’ll collect the results in just a moment.

2. User action
Participants share their smiley reactions + optional comment

3. Facilitator closure
Thank you all for participating in this retrospective. Your input is what makes these sessions meaningful, and I appreciate the time and effort you've put into reflecting and sharing. Let's use what we've learned today to keep improving!

## UI/UX Design Decisions

Based on the system-ui mockups, the retrospective interface follows these design principles:

### Layout Structure
- **Golden header bar**: Clean progress indicators for 5 stages with completion status (Complete/In Progress/To Do)
- **Three-column layout**:
  - **Left sidebar**: Video/guidance area with overlay tooltips containing step-by-step facilitation instructions
  - **Center area**: Clean, focused main content for activities (minimal clutter)
  - **Right sidebar**: Participants list and results display (categorical responses, action points)

### Guidance System
- **Embedded facilitation coaching**: The app acts as the facilitator, with human facilitator just clicking "Next"
- **Flexible tooltip format**: Support for text, video, or audio guidance (starting with text)
- **Contextual instructions**: Guidance appears in small overlays, not cluttering the main interface
- **Quality boost without annoyance**: Detailed enough to improve facilitation quality, limited enough to avoid too many pages

### Design Philosophy
- **Clean and straightforward**: Based on system-ui mockups with professional appearance
- **App-driven facilitation**: Reduces need for certified scrum master knowledge while maintaining high retrospective quality
- **Minimal cognitive load**: Clear visual hierarchy, focused content areas, embedded guidance

### Technical Implementation
- Templates render as fragments within existing layout.html structure
- Stage progress dynamically updates based on actual retrospective template data
- Guidance tooltips support multiple media types for future flexibility
- Right sidebar adapts content based on current activity type (participants vs results)
